About Me:

My early passions for science and math and their intersections with medicine drove me to study biomedical engineering at the Georgia Institute of Technology. It was during an introductory biostatistics course that I fell in love with statistics, and knew immediately that it was something I wanted to study for the rest of my life. Statistics appeals to me because it combines all of my passions for math, computer science, and medicine like no other subject I have encountered. It was for this reason that I now pursue my PhD in Statistics at Rice University.

I currently conduct research under the advisement of Dr. Christine Peterson at MD Anderson. My current project concerns improving the quality of CT scan images taken of cancer patients. Computed Tomography (CT) is often used to diagnose and treat patients with cancer. However, CT scans are not always of ideal quality for radiologists to make accurate judgements for the patient. For example, if there is not enough contrast in the image, then the scan has to be retaken at a later date. This means another trip to the hospital for patients, more time spent per patient for technicians, and an overall strain on hospital resources. I have proposed and am currently constructing a machine learning model that can be used to predict where various venous structures are, so that we may measure its mean pixel value. From this, a decision can be made by technicians on whether or not the image is of good enough quality.

My ultimate goal is to continue exploring my passion for using scientific and computational principles to solve pressing biomedical data problems. I love working with patient data, and a PhD in statistics will give me the necessary mathematical expertise to address the explosion of biomedical data and improve health outcomes across the globe in all areas of medicine. My long-term aspiration is to conduct independent statistics research in academia, government, or industry, so that I can freely explore bleeding-edge methods that have the potential to improve the human condition.
